Summary:

The book provides a practical, jargon-free guide to stock market trading and investing. The author, Robbie Burns, shares his personal experiences, strategies and lessons learned over 10+ years of successful trading.
Key topics covered include: getting set up to trade, understanding basic market mechanics, researching and selecting shares, various trading strategies, using tools like stop losses and profit targets, managing risk, and avoiding common mistakes.
The author emphasizes the importance of simplicity, patience, and disciplined risk management. He cautions against gambling mindsets, over-trading, and relying on "magic" systems or tips.
Specific strategies discussed include: finding companies undergoing positive change, playing sector rotations, recovering plays, and using chart patterns/breakouts. The author also covers important trading psychology concepts.
The book shares many real-life trading stories, both successes and failures, to illustrate key lessons. It provides a solid foundation for beginner and intermediate traders looking to improve their skills and results.

Chapter 1 Summary: Welcome to the Crazy World of Shares

The author introduces the book as a guide for making money from buying and selling shares, regardless of age or background. He promises to explain stock market concepts in plain English, without complex jargon.
The author emphasizes that this is not a "get rich quick" book. Rather, it focuses on building wealth slowly and steadily through disciplined investing. He acknowledges that shares can be volatile, with both ups and downs.
Key points:

The author quit his job in 2001 to trade shares full-time, and now enjoys a relaxed lifestyle.
He cautions against relying on "tips" or systems that promise big profits with no effort. Sustainable gains require patience and work.
The author's trading style is more medium-term than day trading. He does not spend all day glued to the screen.
Readers are encouraged to start slowly, use stop losses, and not risk more than they can afford to lose.

Chapter 2 Summary: Getting Started
This chapter provides a comprehensive guide to the practical steps of getting set up to trade shares. The author walks the reader through each essential element, ensuring a solid foundation before progressing to more advanced topics. 

Chapter 3 Summary: The Knowledge
This chapter delves into the essential information and concepts a new trader needs to understand before entering the stock market.

Spreads:
The author explains the difference between the bid price (sell price) and the offer/ask price (buy price), known as the "spread." He emphasizes that this spread represents a real cost of trading that must be accounted for. The author advises keeping an eye on the percentage size of the spread, avoiding shares with spreads over 5%.

Normal Market Size (NMS):
The author explains that for many smaller company shares, there is a minimum number of shares that market makers are willing to buy or sell at the quoted prices. This "normal market size" is important to be aware of, as dealing in larger quantities may result in less favorable pricing.

Trading Hours:
The London Stock Exchange operates between 8:00 am and 4:30 pm. The author cautions that spreads can be wider and liquidity lower during the first and last minutes of the trading day.

Why Shares Move:
The author provides an extensive list of potential factors that can cause a stock's price to rise or fall, including broker upgrades/downgrades, general market movements, sector trends, company news, and more. He emphasizes the importance of understanding the context behind price changes.

Market Makers and the "Tree Shake":
The author explains the role of market makers in providing liquidity and setting bid/ask prices. He cautions that market makers may sometimes deliberately try to "shake out" investors by rapidly dropping a stock's price, only to see it rebound shortly after.

Chapter 4 Summary: Trading Strategies

Market Timing:
The author emphasizes the critical importance of proper market timing when entering a trade. He advises against simply buying a "good" company without considering the current market conditions and momentum. Key factors to assess include:

The overall trend of the stock - is it gradually rising or falling?
The trading volume and whether there is momentum building
Upcoming news or events that could impact the share price

The author stresses the need to have a clear plan for each trade, including defined entry and exit points. He encourages the use of stop-loss orders to limit potential losses.
Setting Stop Losses and Profit Targets:
The author goes in-depth on the effective use of stop losses and profit targets. He provides examples of how to identify appropriate price levels based on support/resistance and chart patterns.
The author cautions against the common mistakes of holding losing trades too long and taking profits too quickly. He believes allowing winners to run while cutting losses promptly is key to long-term success.
IPOs (Initial Public Offerings):
The author views new IPOs as a potentially fertile hunting ground for profitable trades. He explains his process for identifying the most promising new stock listings, favoring those in high-growth sectors with a solid business model.
The author notes the important distinction between main market IPOs, which tend to be more established, versus riskier AIM market offerings. He provides several examples of successful IPO trades he has executed.
Seasonal Trading Patterns:
The author has observed consistent seasonal trends in the stock market, which he aims to capitalize on. For example, he often looks to buy the FTSE 100 index in early/mid-December and sell in early January to take advantage of the "Santa Rally."
The author also discusses potentially profitable trading strategies around the performance of retail stocks leading up to and following the Christmas holiday period.

Chapter 5 Summary: More Knowledge
Spread Betting:
The author devotes a substantial section to explaining the mechanics and benefits of spread betting as an alternative to traditional stock trading. Key points covered include:

The tax-free nature of spread betting gains
The ability to profit from falling share prices through "shorting"
The use of leverage and margin, which can amplify both profits and losses
Strategies for managing risk, such as stop-loss orders and position sizing

Spread Betting Explained
The core concept behind spread betting is that instead of directly buying and selling shares in the stock market, you are placing "bets" on whether the price of a stock will go up or down. You don't actually own the underlying shares - you are speculating on the future price movements.
The key benefits of spread betting that the author highlights are:

Tax-Free Profits: Any gains made from spread betting are exempt from capital gains tax, unlike regular stock trading. This can result in significant tax savings, especially for more active traders.
Trading on Margin: Spread betting firms allow traders to use leverage, often providing access to 10x or even 20x the initial capital. This can amplify both profits and losses.
Shorting Opportunities: With spread betting, it's just as easy to profit from a stock price decline as it is from a rise. Traders can speculate on share prices going down by taking "short" positions.
Broader Instrument Selection: Beyond individual stocks, spread betting platforms give access to trading a wide range of other instruments like indices, currencies, commodities, and more.

The author cautions that while these features can be very appealing, they also come with significant risks that must be carefully managed. Specifically:

Wider Spreads: Spread betting firms charge a wider gap between the buy and sell prices compared to regular stock trades. This eats into potential profits.
Longer-Term Trades are Costly: For positions held over multiple months, the periodic "rolling over" of the bet can incur additional spread costs.
Unlimited Losses: With leverage, potential losses are not limited to the trader's initial stake. This makes proper risk management absolutely critical.

The author advises that spread betting is best suited for more experienced traders who fully understand the mechanics and risks involved. For beginners, he recommends starting with regular stock trading first to build a solid foundation.
Example Spread Bet Trade
To help illustrate how spread betting works in practice, the author walks through a detailed example trade:
Suppose the current market price of a stock is 500-501 (the bid-ask spread). A spread bettor decides they think the stock price will rise. They can place a "buy" spread bet at 505.
If the stock price does indeed rise to, say, 515-520, the trader can then "sell" the bet at 515, locking in a 10-point profit. On a £10 per point stake, that would equate to a £100 gain.
Conversely, if the stock price fell to 495-500, the trader could "sell" the bet at 495, resulting in a £100 loss (505 - 495 = 10 points x £10 per point).
The author stresses the importance of setting appropriate stop-loss orders to limit potential downside, as well as only risking a small percentage of one's overall capital on any single spread bet.

The author provides several examples to illustrate spread betting in action, while also cautioning about the dangers of over-leveraging and the potential for heavy losses if not managed properly.

Chapter 6 Summary: Trading in Real Life
This chapter delves into the real-world challenges and mistakes that traders often encounter, drawing on the author's own experiences as well as stories shared by his readers. The goal is to help the reader avoid common pitfalls and cultivate the right mindset for long-term success.
Avoiding the "Stinkers"

The key lesson here is the importance of thoroughly researching a company's financials, particularly its debt levels, before investing. The author emphasizes that taking the time to do this basic due diligence can help traders avoid getting caught in disastrous "stinker" stocks that end up going bust.
To drive this point home, the author walks through a simple "back of the envelope" rule he uses - if a company's net debt exceeds three times its annual pre-tax profits, he considers it too risky to invest in. This easy-to-apply guideline has served him well over the years.
Mistakes Made by New Investors
The author then shifts focus to the most common mistakes he has observed among newer traders, drawing from the many emails and messages he has received over the years. Some of the key missteps include:

Treating trading like gambling rather than investing - buying into "penny stocks" or other highly speculative investments without proper research.
Failing to set stop-loss orders, which allows losing positions to spiral out of control.
Letting emotions like greed and fear drive trading decisions, rather than sticking to a disciplined strategy.
Overtrading by making too many trades, which can quickly eat away at profits through commissions and spread costs.

The author emphasizes that developing the right psychology and temperament is just as important as mastering technical analysis when it comes to successful trading. He shares examples of his own past mistakes, like getting emotionally attached to a losing position, to illustrate how easy it is to fall into these traps.
Traders' Tales
To further drive home these lessons, the author includes a section featuring real-life trading horror stories contributed by his readers. These cautionary tales cover a wide range of mistakes, from blindly following tips to letting losses snowball out of control.
By sharing these raw, unvarnished accounts, the author aims to help the reader recognize common pitfalls and avoid repeating the same errors. The stories also serve to normalize the fact that even experienced traders make mistakes - the key is learning from them and adjusting one's approach accordingly.
Throughout this chapter, the overarching message is that successful trading requires a combination of methodical research, disciplined risk management, and the right mindset. The author repeatedly stresses the importance of cutting losses quickly, running profits, and not getting caught up in the thrill of chasing quick gains.

Chapter 7 Summary: The End (Almost)
This final chapter of "The Naked Trader" brings together the key lessons and principles the author has shared throughout the book. It serves as a concise reference guide for readers to revisit as they continue their stock trading journey.
The Naked Trader Rules
The author presents a comprehensive list of 40 "rules" for successful trading. These cover a wide range of topics, including:
Risk Management:

Only trade with money you can afford to lose
Avoid getting emotionally attached to losing trades
Use tight stop-loss orders to cut losses quickly

Stock Selection:

Thoroughly research a company before buying
Avoid "penny stocks" and very small companies
Be wary of buying shares just because they've fallen a lot

Trading Mindset:

Don't overtrade or get overconfident after a winning streak
Patience is key - the best trades may require waiting
Diversify your portfolio to manage risk

The Naked Trader's 40 Trading Rules

1. Only play with money you can really afford to lose. Be honest with yourself.
The author emphasizes the importance of only trading with money you can comfortably afford to lose. He cautions against risking funds you need for essential expenses or that would cause serious financial hardship if lost. Being fully honest with yourself about your risk tolerance is crucial.

2. Get the whole story about a company before you buy. Learn everything about it.
Thorough research is essential before investing in any company. The author advises digging into the business, its financials, market position, growth potential, and any risks or negatives - not just focusing on the positives.

3. Don't rush into anything or chase a share price. There's always another share coming along in a minute.
The author stresses the need for patience. He warns against impulsively buying shares just because the price is rising, as this can lead to buying at inflated levels. The market will always provide new opportunities, so there's no need to rush.

9. Don't take profits too quickly. If you've got a good share and it's slowly heading up, stick with it. Run your winners.
In contrast to rule 8, the author advocates for letting profitable trades "run" rather than hastily taking gains. He believes allowing winning positions to continue rising is key to building wealth over time.

10. Don't be panicked out of a good share if it goes down for a day. The market makers may be trying to get your shares cheaply.
The author cautions against selling a sound investment just because the share price dips temporarily. He explains that market makers may deliberately try to manipulate prices lower to scare traders into selling at unfavorable levels.

11. Beware of buying a share just because a director bought some. They often get it wrong. Some shares have gone bust two weeks after a director buy.
While director share purchases can provide useful information, the author warns that they are not a reliable signal to blindly follow. He has seen many cases where director buying did not translate into positive returns.

12. If you are buying more than £3,000 of a small share, check its normal market size. If that is below £1,000 of shares, you may have trouble when it comes to selling them.
The author highlights the importance of understanding a company's "normal market size" - the typical trading volume that market makers will quote prices for. Exceeding this can lead to difficulties when trying to exit a position.

13. Never catch a falling knife. That means don't buy a share because it's gone down a lot. Better to buy shares that are going up. That bargain may end up even cheaper.
The author strongly advises against trying to "bottom pick" by buying shares that have already suffered significant declines. He believes it's better to focus on shares demonstrating positive momentum.

14. Read! Buy investment books and then buy some more. You can never learn too much.
The author emphasizes the importance of continuous learning for traders. He encourages readers to avidly read books and resources to continually expand their knowledge and skills.

15. Don't buy a share after a profit warning (like London buses, they often come in threes).
The author cautions against buying a stock that has just issued a profit warning. He believes such negative news is often a precursor to further bad news and continued price declines.

16. Check the spread between the buying and selling prices. If it's more than 5%, the share could be far too risky.
As discussed in earlier chapters, the author views wide bid-ask spreads as a red flag indicating a potentially illiquid and high-risk stock. He recommends avoiding shares with spreads over 5%.

17. Beware of buying before 9am - the spreads can be at their widest. Don't be suckered into buying a share at a silly price early on.
The author cautions that shares can experience wider than normal spreads at the market open, warning traders against rushing to buy at unfavorable prices during this period.

18. Don't buy and sell indices like the FTSE 100 or the Dow, especially if you are a new investor. That is just gambling.
The author strongly discourages new traders from attempting to trade major market indices directly, viewing it as high-risk speculation rather than sound investing.

19. Be careful of mining and oil exploration stocks. One bad report can see these shares tumble and they are impossible to value properly.
The author considers mining and oil exploration companies to be particularly speculative and volatile investments, with their values highly sensitive to news and difficult to accurately assess.

20. Don't buy companies that are making a loss. You're gambling they'll make a profit one day. It may never happen.
The author advises against investing in companies that are currently unprofitable, as he views this as a high-risk bet that profitability will be achieved in the future - an outcome that is far from guaranteed.

21. Don't buy a share because it has a perk, such as discounted hotel rooms.
The author dismisses share "perks" like discounted products or services as an irrelevant consideration when selecting investments. He believes traders should focus solely on the underlying business fundamentals.

22. Use all the credit/leverage you can get hold of when spread betting. (This rule is intentionally wrong.)
This rule is deliberately incorrect, as the author strongly cautions against overusing leverage when spread betting, as it can dramatically amplify losses.

23. Use stop losses. Set them around 10% away from your buying price and don't hesitate to act on them.
The author emphasizes the critical importance of setting stop-loss orders to limit potential losses, recommending levels around 10% from the entry price.

24. Try to have a plan with every share. Think about what your exit price will be.
The author stresses the need to approach each trade with a clear plan, including defined profit targets and exit points, rather than trading reactively.

25. Shares are for buying and selling. Do run profits but also take them sometimes.
The author advocates for a balanced approach, neither stubbornly holding onto profitable positions indefinitely nor hastily snatching up small gains.

26. Don't get involved with things you don't understand. So don't buy CFDs or covered warrants if you don't fully understand them.
The author cautions against traders venturing into complex derivative instruments like CFDs and covered warrants unless they have a thorough grasp of how they work and the associated risks.

27. Beware of trading too much on margin. Think about what your losses could be and whether you can afford to cover them.
The author warns against overleveraging positions through excessive use of margin, as this can lead to outsized losses that exceed one's capital.

28. If you're losing heavily it may be best to cut all your positions and come back to the market another time.
The author suggests that when facing significant losses, it may be prudent to completely exit the market for a cooling-off period rather than doubling down.

29. Don't over-trade. Keep to about 8–10 open positions. Any more and you are going to have trouble keeping on top of them.
The author believes traders should limit the number of concurrent positions to around 8-10, as managing a larger portfolio can become unwieldy and increase the risk of mistakes.

30. Beware of overconfidence. If you're on a winning run don't start increasing the size of your positions.
The author cautions against the tendency for traders to become overconfident and increase their position sizes after a string of successful trades, as this can lead to outsized losses if the market turns against them.

31. Don't only go long, do think about shorting stocks, but this is not for beginners.
While the author believes shorting can be a valuable strategy, he advises that it is not suitable for novice traders and should only be attempted once one has gained sufficient experience.

32. Be careful about relying on one method of stock picking. Cover all the angles.
The author recommends that traders employ a diverse toolkit of research and analysis techniques, rather than relying solely on a single approach like technical analysis or fundamentals.

33. Watch for tipping points. Don't lose control of your investments and stay calm.
The author warns about the dangers of emotional "tipping points" where traders can become overwhelmed and lose control of their trading discipline. Maintaining composure is crucial.

34. It's fun to gamble, pick a stock at random sometimes. (This rule is intentionally wrong.)
Contrary to this rule, the author strongly advises against randomly "gambling" on stocks without proper research and planning, viewing it as a surefire path to losses.

35. Always check your ex-dividend dates. Your share will fall on this date for the amount of the dividend, so don't panic.
The author emphasizes the importance of being aware of ex-dividend dates, as share prices tend to drop by the dividend amount on that day, which is not an indication of broader weakness.

36. Open more than one broking account. Then you can compare their services and systems.
The author recommends maintaining accounts with multiple brokers to allow for comparison of services, fees, and trading capabilities, providing more flexibility.

37. Don't rely solely on charts, but certainly use them – along with other tools – to help your trading decisions.
While the author believes charts can provide valuable insights, he cautions against relying on them exclusively, advocating for a multi-faceted approach that incorporates various analytical techniques.

38. Read the financial press – some of it is rubbish but you must keep in touch with what's going on.
The author encourages traders to stay informed by regularly consuming financial news and reports, while remaining discerning about the quality and reliability of the information sources.

39. Always "average down" and buy more shares in one you're already losing badly on as you may end up breaking even. (This rule is intentionally wrong.)
Contrary to this rule, the author strongly advises against the common mistake of "averaging down" on losing positions, as this can lead to compounding losses.

40. Remember the good advice of Corporal Jones in Dad's Army: "Don't panic!"
The author emphasizes the importance of maintaining composure and avoiding panic when faced with market volatility or losing trades, drawing an analogy to the famous character from the sitcom "Dad's Army".

By presenting these 40 rules, the author aims to distill the key lessons and principles he has shared throughout the book into a concise, easily referenced format. The inclusion of several intentionally "wrong" rules encourages the reader to think critically about each guideline rather than simply memorizing them.

Overall, these rules encompass essential aspects of risk management, stock selection, trading psychology, and developing a well-rounded, disciplined approach – all of which are critical for achieving long-term success in the stock market.

Final Thoughts
The chapter concludes with some parting wisdom from the author. He reiterates the core message that successful trading is more about patience and methodical research than chasing get-rich-quick schemes.
The author cautions against overthinking or overanalyzing, using the example of a conversation with an accountant who had become paralyzed by trying to perfect every financial metric. Instead, the author advocates for a balanced, commonsense approach:
"Don't overdo it. You don't have to analyse everything to the nth degree. My simple methods outlined in this book should work. And you will, in time, work out your own parameters. Simple can be best."
This final message emphasizes the author's belief that stock trading, at its core, is more about developing the right mindset and risk management skills than mastering complex technical analysis. By distilling the essentials and encouraging the reader to find their own trading style, the author hopes to empower the reader to become a consistently profitable trader.
Overall, this concluding chapter ties together the key lessons and principles covered throughout the book. It provides the reader with a concise set of guidelines to internalize, while also reinforcing the importance of adapting these ideas to one's own unique trading personality and goals.
